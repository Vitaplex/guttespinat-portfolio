## Preface
I want to preface this with a contradictory-sounding statement: **Discipline is not something you have!**  
It is a trait you bear from time to time. Discipline will come and go: you will have periods where you feel undisciplined, or very disciplined. It is always in you to do what you must, it's only a matter of whether you're able to see it right now or not.

## What even is discipline?!
Discipline can be a tricky concept to define. It is a socially loaded topic, and people will give varying descriptions of what discipline is. Some people will say "pushing yourself to do something, even though you don't want to". Others might say its "working hard", "being hard on yourself" or "getting shit done!". These are all aspects of discipline, but doesn't give the full picture.  

In my personal experience, discipline has always been about staying with a task despite its struggles, but also learning to enjoy the positive aspects of it, seeing the struggle as a struggle - potential to grow as a person.  
However, there are different types of struggles, lets dive deeper.

## The different types of struggle
Struggle can be seen as resistance; something pushing back at you, either its physical or mental. Like lifting weights at the gym, or writing up an essay for a school assignment. These are both struggles, things which demand something from you, in a sense. If you want optimal results, you have to put effort into it.  

### The fight of struggles and effort
So effort can be seen as the antidote to struggle, something that fights it. You fight a struggle with effort, and the result is that you have produced something of value; either a worked-out body from the gym, or a written essay. The formula here is dead simple:
```
struggle * effort = product
```
Effort often requires motivation, an intrinsic urge to do something. This urge can come from both bad and good things:  
You can force yourself to do something out of spite, regret, anger or hate. This can be labeled as **negatively motivated struggle**. Then there is **positively motivated struggle**, in which you do something out of curiosity, enjoyment or because you know it's good for you.

### How struggle ties into discipline

So, discipline is cultivated by struggle, resisting the urge to give up. Discipline can be seen as  
"*The mindset that appears in you, as a result of successfully and healthily resisting the urge to give up on a task you should be doing.*"  

But how do you resist your urges in a healthy way?  
As mentioned in the section above, there needs to be the proper mindset behind the struggle - "struggling" with purpose and intent - with positively motivated struggle - **generates** a disciplinary mindset.  

Let's look more into the motivated struggles, with some examples.

### Firstly, there is the bad struggle <sub><i>(Negatively motivated struggle)</i></sub>
This is the struggle that does not generate discipline, but rather generates suffering. You hate the task youre doing - you are suffering. You're "hating your way to success".  
This suffering arises from thoughts - as a result of your mental image of youself, how you view yourself in your situation.  
You think you should do better. You might feel small, inadequate or pathetic. These are thoughts that appear out of comparison to others, out of contrasts, and thus, self-criticism ensues.  
> Look at me, look how *contrasting* I am compared to everyone else - I suck at this, and everyone else is so good. I have been trying this for ages, and I still manage to screw it up!

This can sometimes even be a toxic spiral, where your thoughts perpetuate your self-image, which perpetuate your suffering and **hate**.  

#### On Hate
**Hate** is a feeling. It is the response to a situation bound with a negative context. Hate needs this context to exist. A situation never generates hate on itself. It is the mindset and context around the situation that creates the hate, thus creating the bad struggle - associating a task with hateful thoughts about it or oneself.  
Hate is never inherited, it is conditioned - part of "the conditioned mind", if you will.  

This bears truth in the context of tasks: you cannot hate a task you havent done. You can think you will hate it, but that's, again, your conditioning speaking. Then, you are also comparing and gaugeing the boredom/hateability of a task based on your previous experiences.  

### Secondly, there is good struggle <sub><i>(Positively motivated struggle)</i></sub>
The good struggle is the struggle that simply puts your conditioning aside for a moment, focusing on the practicalities around what needs to be done, instead of ruminating on how much of a drag this task will be. Instead, try to shut the chattering in your mind out for a minute, and think: 
> What do i need to do, **right now**?

Breaking down bigger tasks into smaller tasks helps.  
Usually, the answer to what you need to do is something fairly simple...
>>>> ##### **Example - Cleaning The Back Yard:**
**Cleaning the back yard** - this will take forever  
**Raking the leaves** - this is manageable  
**Moving the rake** - moving a stick with prongs is not hard

Even if you initially feel hate, being open-minded and attempting to be curious, rather than judgemental, can show itself to be a powerful stance when tackling new tasks.  
Confusion, or not knowing where to start, is also another factor. This is where the mind is clouded, either by the amount of options present, or the abstractness of the task. Doing something without knowledge requires research. Starting by gathering context on the topic or task ahead will give you a better understanding of what needs to be done.

Positively motivated struggles can also be seen in relation to the Zen concept of Shoshin, or "beginners mind" - for further reading...

### How doing hard stuff makes you grow
When you live life on easy mode - taking shortcuts, giving little effort to tasks that bore you - you build a certain "tolerance" towards things that require more effort. Things that do not fit your neat bubble of "who you are and what you do" seem monumental, since the span of difficulty in the tasks you do on a day-to-day basis is so low.  
Take this graph depicting the day of two different people - **Blue** lives a life of every day challenges and obstacles to overcome, while **Turquoise** cruises along, only doing what seems fitting at the time, and at their own pace.

![](/static/media/images/inmind/beingdisciplined/chart.png)  

**Blue**, the person that does hard stuff, arguably has a better perspective on what is seen as a hard task. What seems hard for **Turquoise** is small and manageable for **Blue**.  

The growth that comes from doing hard stuff is in the form of **perspective**.
